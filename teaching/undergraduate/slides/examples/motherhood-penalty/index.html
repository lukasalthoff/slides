The Motherhood Penalty After Flexibility

Working from home could help mothers stay in the labor force, or it could quietly cost them by making them look less committed to employers. Which effect wins? This project asks whether remote work is ultimately better or worse for mothers, measured by whether they stay in the workforce longer than they would without access to remote work.

The puzzle

Working from home pulls on mothers in two directions at once. Which one wins?

The motherhood penalty describes the drop in earnings, employment, hours, or advancement that many women experience after having children. Classic research shows that the arrival of children is a major point where men’s and women’s labor market paths split. After COVID made remote work mainstream, a sharper question follows: does working from home protect mothers, or does it open a new way to penalize them?

On one side, working from home may help mothers of young children. It cuts commute time, expands the set of jobs that are feasible to hold, and makes it easier to respond when something urgent happens at home. Each of these can help a mother stay employed instead of dropping out.

On the other side, working from home may hurt mothers. Employers may monitor remote workers more closely, make stronger assumptions about their productivity, or read a mother working from home as less committed precisely because she is a mother. Those judgments can cost her promotions, wages, and career progression.

So the core question is: which effect dominates—is working from home ultimately better or worse for mothers? This project defines “better” as job retention: whether a mother stays attached to paid work. Wages and promotion are treated as supporting signals of how durable that attachment is.

The old penalty

Motherhood has never been one mechanism.

The penalty can begin with employer assumptions: mothers may be seen as less committed even when their qualifications are identical to other workers. It can continue through labor supply, when care responsibilities reduce hours, increase interruptions, or make demanding jobs harder to sustain.

It also lives inside job design. Claudia Goldin’s idea of greedy work describes jobs that reward long, unpredictable, or on-call hours more than proportionally. If one parent must be on call at home, and one parent can be on call at work, families have a financial incentive to specialize.

The post-COVID question is not just whether mothers can keep working. It is whether flexible work lets them keep advancing.

The COVID turn

Working from home changed the location of work. It did not erase the demands of care.

Remote and hybrid work can increase schedule control, reduce commute time, and make it easier to respond to family needs. That matters. A mother who can stay employed through a difficult caregiving period may avoid the sharpest form of labor market exit.

But flexibility has a paradox. If mothers are more likely to use it for caregiving, while fathers or nonparents use it for focus time, then the same policy can preserve employment while still weakening career growth. The penalty becomes less about leaving work altogether and more about fragmented attention, missed informal networks, and fewer visible signals of availability.

The workplace channel

How remote work could turn against mothers

This project focuses on the relationship between a remote-working mother and her employer, managers, and coworkers. These are the four ways that relationship can either keep her attached to her job or push her out.

Monitoring

Surveillance

Remote workers can be tracked more closely through logins, activity software, and message logs. Mothers may absorb the heaviest share of that scrutiny.

Visibility

Face time to screen time

Office presence may matter less, but being out of sight can weaken access to managers, informal networks, and the visible signals of availability that careers run on.

Perception

Commitment doubts

A mother working from home may be assumed to be less productive or less committed, even when her output is identical to a coworker in the office.

Growth

Promotion and wages

If managers reward presence and read remote mothers as less invested, the cost shows up in slower promotion, weaker raises, and a more fragile hold on the job itself.

Evidence map

The literature points toward a flexibility paradox.

Child penalties remain large.

Research on child penalties shows that first births are a major turning point in women’s earnings, hours, and employment relative to men.

Place and norms matter.

Child penalties vary sharply across geography and cultural groups, suggesting that policy alone cannot explain the full pattern.

Career systems reward timing.

Evidence from women in science shows that mothers can remain highly productive, but often on a later timetable than tenure and promotion systems reward.

Flexibility may have uneven returns.

Remote and hybrid work can reduce some costs of care, but their benefits depend on who has access, who uses them, and how managers evaluate output.

Methodology

How this project answers the question.

The plan is quantitative, built on existing datasets rather than new surveys or interviews. The goal is to compare job retention for mothers of young children who work remotely against otherwise similar mothers who work on-site, and to see how that comparison changed as remote work expanded after COVID. A first cut of this data already powers the Remote Work Reality Check below, using real Census ACS microdata restricted to remote-capable occupations (Dingel & Neiman 2020) so job type does not drive the comparison.

Primary outcome: retention

Whether a mother stays employed and attached to the labor force from one period to the next. Wages and job changes serve as supporting outcomes.

Key comparison

Mothers of young children in remote-capable jobs versus on-site jobs, before and after the post-2020 jump in remote work, to separate the effect of working from home from broader trends.

Candidate data sources

ACS and CPS for work-at-home and telework status plus employment; ATUS for time use and where work happens; SIPP for job transitions and fertility; the Household Pulse Survey for the COVID period; and the SWAA remote-work survey for attitudes and arrangements.

What would settle it

If remote mothers show higher retention than comparable on-site mothers, working from home helps. If they leave jobs faster or stall out, the workplace channel is imposing a hidden penalty. The matched CPS panel below runs exactly this test.

Data and method

Shares are computed from the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey 1-year Public Use Microdata Sample (2023), weighted by person weight. The universe is people employed and at work in the reference week who reported a commute mode and who work in a remote-capable occupation; "worked from home" is that commute response. Remote-capable occupations are the major groups that are predominantly teleworkable following Dingel & Neiman (2020), which removes most of the white-collar versus blue-collar confound so the comparison reflects who uses remote work, not just who could. "Young child" means living in a household with an own child under age 6; "bachelor's or higher" is the person's educational attainment; "married" is currently married (MAR = 1) and "unmarried" is not currently married (widowed, divorced, separated, or never married). The numbers are generated by code/fetch_wfh_data.py and stored in website/data/wfh.js.

What the prevalence implies (from the literature):

  • Even among remote-capable jobs, use is uneven. Once job type is held roughly constant, remaining gaps by sex, motherhood, and education reflect who actually takes up remote work.
  • Remote work raises retention but cuts promotion. Bloom, Liang, Roberts & Ying (2015) found working from home cut attrition by over 50% yet roughly halved promotion rates.
  • Distance erodes advancement. Emanuel & Harrington (2023/24) and Emanuel, Harrington & Pallais (2023) find remote workers are promoted less and lose in-person mentoring, with larger effects for women and junior workers.
  • Mothers and remote work. Collins, Landivar, Ruppanner & Scarborough (2021) and Goldin (2014; 2021) link flexibility to whether mothers stay attached to paid work, and at what career cost.

The answer · real panel data

On the retention margin, remote work helps mothers stay employed.

To move past who uses remote work and test what it does, this project follows the same workers over time. The Current Population Survey re-interviews households a year apart, so workers employed in June 2024 can be matched to their own record 12 months later. The question: of those employed at the start, how many are still employed a year on—and does teleworking at the start change the odds?

+2.8 pp

Mothers of young children who teleworked were +2.8 points more likely to still be employed a year later (93.7% vs 90.9%)—a retention gain about three times the one fathers got.

Read with care. The significance test is a simple two-proportion z-test on unweighted matched counts: it ignores the CPS complex survey design and tests many groups at once, so a stray "significant" result is possible by chance. Groups with too few teleworkers to compare (under 100 matched) are omitted.

The argument. On the outcome this project defines as "better"—staying attached to paid work—the evidence points one way: among comparable workers, mothers of young children who work from home are more likely to keep their jobs a year later than mothers who do not, and the boost they get is larger than fathers'. The "remote work keeps mothers employed" story dominates the "remote work pushes mothers out" story on retention. That is consistent with the literature's split verdict: flexibility tends to protect employment even where it dents promotion (Bloom et al. 2015; Emanuel & Harrington 2023/24). The motherhood penalty does not vanish—it narrows on the margin remote work touches most.

How firm is this? It is a strong, defensible association, not a clean causal estimate. Teleworkers differ from on-site workers in occupation, schedule, and unobserved ways, so part of the gap reflects selection. The panel matches 66,000 workers across June 2024–April 2025 at a 60% match rate (typical for CPS), weighted by the baseline person weight. The honest conclusion is a qualified one: remote work is associated with higher retention for mothers, the project's defined measure of "better," with promotion and wage effects left as the open question.

Data and method (retention panel)

Built from the U.S. Census Bureau's Current Population Survey basic monthly public-use files. CPS uses a 4-8-4 rotation, so a person in month-in-sample 1–4 returns 12 months later in month-in-sample 5–8. Workers employed at baseline (PEMLR = 1) who answered the telework item (PTTLWK, available June 2024+) are matched to their record 12 months later on household and person identifiers (HRHHID, HRHHID2, PULINENO), validated on sex and age. "Still employed" is PEMLR 1–2 at follow-up; "teleworked" is PTTLWK = 1 at baseline; "young child" is an own child under 6 (PRCHLD); ages 18–50. The numbers are generated by code/fetch_cps_retention.py and stored in website/data/retention.js.

What this project asks

Does working from home keep mothers employed, or push them out?

If working from home helps mothers stay attached to paid work, that is a real gain. But if employer monitoring, productivity assumptions, and doubts about commitment cost mothers their footing, then remote work has not removed the motherhood penalty—it has relocated it inside the home office. The matched CPS panel above gives a first answer: on retention, the gain wins—mothers who work from home are more likely to keep their jobs a year later. The open question is whether that durable foothold comes at the price of slower promotion and weaker wage growth, which is where the workplace channel could still be hiding the penalty.

Scope note. Remote work can reach mothers through two channels. The first is the workplace channel: the relationship between a mother and her employer, managers, and coworkers, including monitoring, perceptions of productivity, commitment, and promotion. The second is the household channel: being physically at home can raise expectations that the mother also handles childcare, cooking, cleaning, school pickup, and emergencies while working. These are related but distinct stories. This project analyzes the workplace channel; the household channel is left for future work.

Conclusion

What this project shows, and where it goes next.

01 / Done

What I did

I reframed the post-COVID motherhood penalty as a single question—does working from home help mothers stay in the workforce or quietly push them out?—and defined "better" as staying employed. I then built two tools on real federal microdata: a calculator of who actually works from home (Census ACS, by sex, young children, education, and marital status) and a 12-month matched CPS panel that measures whether workers keep their jobs, broken down by occupation.

02 / Seen

What I found

Among comparable workers, mothers of young children who work from home are about 2.8 points more likely to still be employed a year later—roughly three times the boost fathers get. That retention gain is largest in lower-retention jobs and near zero where retention is already high (such as computer and mathematical work). And remote-work uptake itself is uneven: it is lower for unmarried mothers and for mothers without a bachelor's degree.

03 / Next

What I'd do next

Retention is only half the story. Next I would extend the panel to promotion and wage growth, where the penalty may still hide; pursue a stronger causal design using the pre- and post-2020 jump in remote work and longitudinal SIPP data; and bring in the household channel—time use and partner expectations—that this project deliberately set aside.

Contribution. The literature splits on flexibility: it tends to protect employment (Bloom et al. 2015) but can slow advancement (Emanuel & Harrington 2023/24). This project contributes by testing the retention side directly on recent, nationally representative panel data, showing the effect is real but concentrated in particular workers and jobs—which sharpens where the remaining motherhood penalty is most likely to live.
